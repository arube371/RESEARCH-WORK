BACKGROUND OF THE STUDY:
Heritage refers to the legacy of physical artefacts and intangible attributes inherited from past generations, maintained in the present, and passed on to future generations (UNESCO, 2021). Heritage encompasses two main categories: tangible heritage, which refers to physical items like monuments, structures, artifacts, and cultural landscapes, and intangible heritage, which includes elements such as traditions, performing arts, rituals, oral traditions, and social customs (ICOMOS, 2019). Heritage acts as a cornerstone for identity, history, and continuity, connecting communities to their historical roots and influencing a sense of shared belonging. 
Heritage preservation encompasses the safeguarding, upkeep, and conservation of both cultural and natural resources to guarantee their longevity and the ability to pass them down through generations (UNESCO, 2022). Globally, the preservation of heritage is achieved through a range of approaches such as recording historical information, restoring ancient sites, creating museums, implementing laws to protect heritage, and involving local communities. As an illustration, Italy and France uphold rigorous regulations regarding cultural heritage and operate public museums dedicated to the preservation of art and architectural works. Meanwhile, Japan advocates for the "Living National Treasures" initiative, which aims to protect intangible cultural practices like traditional craftsmanship. In the realm of performing arts, countries in Africa, such as Kenya and Ghana, are incorporating cultural tourism that is rooted in the community to preserve their local traditions and heritage (ICOMOS, 2019; Borgen Project, 2020). There are numerous reasons to protect heritage sites. They play a crucial role in shaping cultural identity, enhancing educational opportunities, promoting social unity, and acting as a valuable economic asset through tourism (Vaia, 2023). 
Neglecting to protect cultural heritage presents serious difficulties. Around the world, factors such as urban development, warfare, and climate change have resulted in the loss of important cultural treasures (UNESCO, 2021). In Uganda, especially within the Karamoja sub-region, traditional practices are under pressure due to factors such as modernization, changes in climate, displacement of communities, and a reduction in the passing down of knowledge between generations according to the British Council Cultural Protection Fund (2023), the unique architectural styles of manyattas, specific clothing traditions, and the oral histories of the Karamojong and Ik people are gradually fading away as the younger members of these communities move away from these cultural practices embrace contemporary ways of living (Monitor, 2023). Consequently, the issue arises from a diminished sense of identity, a decline in cultural pride, and the possible economic benefits that could be gained from cultural heritage. This could be due to a lack of adequate preservation efforts in areas such as Karamoja. Various elements affect the preservation of heritage. 
These include the extent of community engagement, access to financial and technical resources, supportive legal and policy structures, and education. Understanding the importance of heritage and the impact of tourism, especially cultural tourism, is essential (Rethinking the Future, 2022; Borgen Project, 2020). Cultural tourism emphasizes traveling to places to immerse oneself in their unique cultural experiences. Understanding their culture, traditions, and lifestyle often serves as a source of motivation and financial support for conservation efforts. When handled properly, it can enhance community pride and encourage sustainable practices. This study will examine cultural tourism as a key factor in preserving heritage, while also promoting the protection of cultural sites and practices, as highlighted by UNWTO in 2021. 
Cultural heritage
Cultural heritage includes the common expressions, artifacts, knowledge, and values that communities receive from their forebears and pass down to future generations (UNESCO, 2022). It covers both physical aspects. Cultural heritage includes physical elements like monuments, artifacts, and historical sites, along with non-physical elements such as oral traditions, social practices, and performing arts. It is important to note that cultural heritage is not static; it changes and develops as communities adjust to new circumstances, changing environments, while maintaining essential identity features. In areas like Karamoja, cultural heritage encompasses traditional music and dance, communal living setups, livestock management, storytelling, and Ceremonies play a crucial role in shaping social structures and belief systems. Recognizing cultural heritage is essential for understanding how tourism can help preserve it and promote sustainable development.
Cultural tourism
Cultural tourism refers to travel motivated by an interest in experiencing and understanding the cultural expressions, lifestyles, heritage, and traditions of other people (UNWTO, 2021). It involves visiting destinations where the main attractions are the historical sites, cultural events, local traditions, art, music, dance, language, cuisine, or daily lifestyles of a community. Unlike mass tourism, which focuses mainly on leisure and recreation, cultural tourism emphasizes learning, appreciation, and interaction with local culture. According to UNESCO (2022), cultural tourism is one of the fastest-growing segments of the global tourism industry, contributing significantly to both cultural preservation and economic development.
Cultural tourism occurs in diverse contexts worldwide. In Europe, countries like Italy, France, and Greece attract millions of tourists each year to explore ancient monuments, museums, and festivals that showcase their rich historical heritage. In Asia, nations such as Japan and India use cultural tourism to preserve traditional arts, temples, and crafts through heritage villages and festivals (ICOMOS, 2019). In Africa, cultural tourism is increasingly recognized as a tool for both economic empowerment and heritage preservation. For example, in Kenya, the Maasai Mara and Samburu communities have developed cultural villages that allow visitors to experience traditional dances, crafts, and pastoral lifestyles, while in Ghana and Nigeria, cultural festivals such as the Panafest and Osun-Osogbo Festival attract both domestic and international visitors, reinforcing pride in indigenous traditions (UNWTO, 2021; Borgen Project, 2020).
In Uganda, cultural tourism has become an integral part of national tourism policy, with emphasis on community-based initiatives that highlight traditional music, dance, crafts, and heritage sites. The Karamoja sub-region is a key example, where community tourism enterprises such as Kara-Tunga Arts & Tours promote cultural exchange and heritage preservation by inviting tourists to participate in Karamojong dances, traditional cattle herding, and local cuisine experiences (British Council Cultural Protection Fund, 2023). Such initiatives not only provide income but also motivate communities to maintain and transmit their customs, attire, and architecture that might otherwise fade under modern influences.
Cultural tourism thus serves a dual purpose: it generates economic benefits for host communities while simultaneously encouraging the conservation of their cultural resources. It creates awareness of the importance of heritage, stimulates pride among local people, and provides the financial means to maintain traditional practices and sites. However, it must be managed carefully to avoid the commodification or distortion of cultural expressions. Therefore, this study seeks to investigate the effect of cultural tourism on the preservation of heritage.
Karamoja, located in northeastern Uganda, is one of the country’s most culturally rich areas. It is home to different ethnic groups like the Karimojong, Ik, Tepeth, and Pokot, who practice pastoralism, build manyattas (traditional homes), and celebrate traditional dances, songs, ceremonies, crafts, and dress (Barigaba, 2024). Over the past decade, tourism has grown in Karamoja. Tourists are attracted by the region’s cultural uniqueness and natural beauty, especially Kidepo Valley National Park, rock paintings in Moroto, and cultural festivals like the Karamoja Cultural Festival (Monitor, 2024).

Description of Study Variables
2.1.1 Preservation of Local Heritage
Preservation of local heritage refers to the protection, maintenance, and transmission of cultural assets—both tangible and intangible—from one generation to the next (UNESCO, 2021). Tangible heritage includes monuments, artifacts, sacred sites, and historical structures, while intangible heritage consists of oral traditions, performing arts, rituals, craftsmanship, and social customs (ICOMOS, 2019). The preservation process ensures that cultural identity and memory are not lost amid globalization and modernization (Harrison, 2013). It fosters a sense of belonging, reinforces community pride, and strengthens continuity between past and present generations. In Uganda, efforts toward cultural heritage preservation include the documentation of indigenous knowledge, protection of heritage sites through national laws, and promotion of community-based initiatives that integrate cultural tourism (National Museum of Uganda, 2022).
2.1.2 Cultural Tourism
Cultural tourism is defined by the United Nations World Tourism Organization (UNWTO, 2021) as a form of tourism where the traveller’s primary motivation is to learn, discover, experience, and consume the tangible and intangible cultural attractions or products in a tourism destination. These attractions include arts, heritage, and the lifestyles of people. Cultural tourism emphasizes interaction and appreciation over consumption, creating opportunities for visitors to engage meaningfully with host communities (Richards, 2020). It often includes activities such as attending cultural festivals, visiting heritage sites, learning traditional crafts, and participating in local rituals or performances. In Africa, cultural tourism has become a vehicle for both economic empowerment and the revitalization of indigenous heritage (Bello et al., 2020).
2.1.3 Relationship Between Cultural Tourism and Heritage Preservation
Cultural tourism and heritage preservation share a symbiotic relationship. Tourism can generate revenue and awareness for conservation efforts, while heritage assets provide the foundation for cultural tourism experiences (Timothy & Nyaupane, 2009). When managed responsibly, cultural tourism helps sustain traditional practices by giving them new relevance in the modern world. For example, community-based tourism projects often support local artisans and performers, ensuring that their knowledge and skills are valued and passed on (UNESCO, 2022). However, without careful regulation, tourism may also lead to the commodification or alteration of cultural expressions (McKercher & du Cros, 2020). Thus, understanding this balance is key to promoting sustainable cultural heritage management.

2.2 Preservation of Local Heritage
2.2.1 Concept and Importance of Local Heritage Preservation
The preservation of local heritage is central to cultural identity and societal continuity. According to UNESCO (2021), heritage represents the cumulative legacy of a community’s history, beliefs, and creative expression. Preserving this legacy helps maintain a sense of identity and belonging, ensuring that cultural values and practices are not eroded by external influences such as modernization or globalization. Heritage preservation also contributes to social cohesion by connecting people through shared narratives and symbols (Smith, 2006). Furthermore, heritage preservation serves educational, economic, and environmental purposes. Educationally, it enables younger generations to learn about their origins, fostering respect for diversity and tradition (Graham, Ashworth, & Tunbridge, 2016). Economically, it creates opportunities for tourism and creative industries, while environmentally, the maintenance of traditional architecture and land-use practices often promotes sustainable resource management (Rethinking the Future, 2022). Globally, heritage preservation encompasses various approaches, including documentation, restoration, legal protection, and community participation. Documentation involves recording oral histories, cataloging artifacts, and mapping cultural landscapes (ICOMOS, 2019). Restoration refers to repairing or reconstructing heritage structures while maintaining their authenticity (Feilden, 2003). Legal frameworks, such as UNESCO’s 1972 World Heritage Convention, establish mechanisms for identifying and safeguarding sites of cultural and natural significance. Community participation has gained increasing attention as a cornerstone of sustainable preservation. When residents are actively involved, they become custodians of their heritage rather than passive observers (Meskell, 2018). This participatory model ensures that preservation efforts align with community priorities and knowledge systems. In the African context, local custodianship plays a crucial role, as many traditions are transmitted orally or through performance (Ndoro & Wijesuriya, 2015).
Challenges to Heritage Preservation. Despite its recognized importance, heritage preservation faces multiple challenges. Urbanization and infrastructure development often lead to the destruction or alteration of historical sites. Climate change poses threats to both tangible and intangible heritage, with rising temperatures and extreme weather events damaging monuments and disrupting cultural practices (UNESCO, 2022). In addition, globalization and modernization have led to declining interest in traditional lifestyles among younger generations, resulting in the erosion of indigenous knowledge systems (British Council Cultural Protection Fund, 2023). In Uganda, and particularly in Karamoja, these challenges are pronounced. The Karimojong, Ik, and Tepeth communities face pressures from modernization, shifting settlement patterns, and limited institutional support for cultural preservation (Monitor, 2023). Traditional architecture, such as manyattas, indigenous clothing, and oral histories, is disappearing, weakening cultural continuity. The lack of resources and formal education on heritage management further exacerbates this decline (NEMA Uganda, 2021). Preserving heritage reinforces a community’s collective identity and self-esteem. As Harrison (2013) notes, heritage is not merely about the past but about how societies construct meaning in the present. In regions like Karamoja, where traditional practices shape daily life, maintaining cultural heritage strengthens social bonds and affirms local identity. Ceremonies, dances, and traditional leadership structures serve as mechanisms for transmitting values and knowledge, thereby sustaining social cohesion (Barigaba, 2024). Cultural pride derived from heritage preservation can also promote intercultural understanding and peacebuilding. By valuing their own traditions, communities are better positioned to engage with external cultures from a standpoint of respect and confidence (UNESCO, 2021). This makes heritage preservation not just a cultural imperative but also a social and developmental one.
2.3 Cultural Tourism
2.3.1 Defining Cultural Tourism and Its Scope
As discussed in the conceptual review, cultural tourism involves travel motivated by interest in the cultural expressions, heritage, and lifestyles of host communities (United Nations World Tourism Organization [UNWTO], 2021). It emphasises immersive experiences, learning, and meaningful interaction rather than purely recreational activities (Richards, 2020). Cultural tourism may include visiting heritage sites, participating in local festivals, engaging with traditional crafts, dance, and music, or staying in community-based settings to experience indigenous ways of life.
In the African context, and particularly in lesser-known destinations, cultural tourism is often viewed as a tool for economic empowerment, heritage revitalisation, and sustainable development (Bello et al., 2020). Cultural tourism can be categorised into several types: heritage tourism (visiting historic/monument sites), festival/ event tourism (attending cultural festivals), ethnographic or community based cultural tourism (immersive stays with local communities), gastronomic cultural tourism (local cuisine), creative cultural tourism (crafts, arts), and intangible culture tourism (performing arts, rituals) (Richards & Wilson, 2006; UNWTO, 2021).
In community-based forms, tourists engage with local hosts in their daily life or tradition: for example, staying in a traditional homestead, participating in cattle herding, attending storytelling sessions, or learning craft skills. This level of interaction distinguishes cultural tourism from more passive sightseeing. Tourists are motivated to engage in cultural tourism for a variety of reasons: a desire for authenticity, learning about “otherness”, enriching life experiences, supporting local communities, and developing deeper cultural understanding (McKercher & du Cros, 2020).
Benefits for host communities include: Economic: Diversifying income sources, generating jobs in guiding, crafts, hospitality, and cultural performance. For instance, in the Karamoja sub-region, this is particularly significant given its economic marginalisation. Cultural: Incentivising maintenance of traditions, crafts, and practices; boosting community pride, and raising awareness of heritage among younger generations. Social: Promoting community cohesion, strengthening identity, enabling inter generational transmission of traditions. Environmental/Developmental: When linked with responsible tourism, it can support conservation, alternative livelihoods, and reduce pressures on natural resources.
However, challenges abound: commodification of culture (turning traditions into performances for tourists), loss of authenticity, exploitation or marginalisation of communities, unequal benefit sharing, cultural disruption, and “tourismification” of sacred practices (Angella, 2024; McKercher & du Cros, 2020). For example, a Ugandan study found that cultural tourism had both positive and negative effects: though economic benefits were realised, some traditions were changed to meet tourist expectations. In the Karamoja sub-region, cultural tourism is emerging as a niche opportunity. For example, the annual Karamoja Cultural Festival brings together multiple ethnic groups (Karimojong, Pokot, Ik, Tepeth, etc.) to celebrate their traditions, dress, dance, and food. It serves both cultural and tourism‐promotion functions. A recent community-based tourism initiative via Kara Tunga Arts & Tours and the Karenga Community Wildlife Association (KCWA) is developing a community lodge near Kidepo Valley National Park to offer immersive cultural and nature‐based experiences in Karamoja. Local tourism operators, e.g., Kalya Peace Village, promote cultural tours including visits to manyattas (traditional homesteads), dance performances, and local cuisine in Karamoja. 
These examples show that cultural tourism in Karamoja is moving from the fringes toward being more intentionally developed, blending heritage, community livelihoods, and tourism. Critical Considerations For cultural tourism to be sustainable in Karamoja, a few critical factors must be addressed: Authenticity vs. Marketability: Ensuring traditions are not diluted or staged purely for tourists. Community participation and empowerment: Local communities must lead and benefit. Capacity building and resources: Training guides, managing infrastructure, and marketing. Karamoja lags in tourism infrastructure and funding. Benefit sharing: Ensuring that income from tourism reaches local households and supports heritage preservation. Conservation of heritage and environment: Linking tourism with preservation of cultural assets and landscapes, not degrading them. Policy, regulation, and monitoring: Ensuring tourism adheres to cultural safeguarding standards and aligns with heritage preservation goals.

2.4 Cultural Tourism on the Preservation of Heritage in Karamoja
2.4.1 Theoretical Linkages Between Tourism and Heritage Preservation
As noted earlier, tourism and heritage preservation interact via several pathways: tourism provides both motivation (economic incentive, global recognition) and resources (funds, infrastructure) for heritage preservation, while heritage assets provide the attraction for tourism (Timothy & Nyaupane, 2009). The theory of cultural sustainability suggests that when local traditions are integrated into tourism in respectful ways, they stand a better chance of being transmitted and revitalised (Smith, 2006; Harrison, 2013). Conversely, tourism theory warns of commodification and authenticity loss (MacCannell, 1973; McKercher & du Cros, 2020).
Thus, the effect of cultural tourism on heritage preservation depends on how well tourism is managed, how communities are engaged, and whether cultural integrity is maintained. In Uganda broadly, a recent study by Angella (2024) examined “Impacts of Cultural Tourism on the Preservation of Local Heritage and Traditions in Uganda” and found that while cultural tourism enhanced awareness and economic benefits for communities, there were also instances of tradition modification for tourist appeal (e.g., staged performances, simplified rituals). In Karamoja, The Monitor reports that Karamoja has been labelled a “tourism gem” for its strong cultural identity, with traditional initiation ceremonies, communal living in manyattas, dress, and rituals remaining largely intact. Yet, the article cautions about practices such as female genital mutilation and inheritance customs that remain but are problematic and need phasing out in a tourism heritage context. The newer community tourism hub near Kidepo seeks to link heritage preservation with visitor experiences: “The initiative is designed to preserve arts, crafts, and lifestyle of the Karamojong while generating income for local families.” These examples suggest that cultural tourism in Karamoja has positive effects in raising the profile of heritage, generating funds, and involving local people. But the negatives, such as potential commodification and heritage loss, appear less documented in Karamoja than elsewhere. 
 Positive Effects. Increased awareness and value of heritage: Cultural tourism creates visibility for heritage assets and encourages communities to value their traditions (UNESCO, 2022). In Karamoja, cultural festivals and tourism promotion serve this function. Economic incentive for preservation: The potential to earn income from tourism gives local communities reason to maintain traditional practices, dress, crafts, and architecture (manyattas) rather than abandon them. Community empowerment and capacity building: Tourism initiatives train local youth as guides, performers, and craft makers, thus transferring skills and strengthening the transmission of heritage knowledge. For example, the agro tourism project in Karamoja trained former cattle rustlers in tour guiding and hospitality. Heritage resource mobilisation: Projects such as the repatriation of Karamojong artefacts contribute to heritage restoration and can be leveraged for tourism.  Negative and Risk Effects: Commodification and authenticity loss: When cultural practices are modified to suit tourist tastes, they may lose meaning for the local community or become performances rather than lived traditions. Angella (2024) found this in Ugandan contexts. Unequal benefit distribution: If tourism benefits are captured by non-locals or a few elites, local custodians of heritage may be excluded, reducing motivation for preservation. In Karamoja, funding for cultural tourism is weak – “less than 3% of the district budget” in one district. Cultural intrusion and change: Increased external contact may lead to younger generations abandoning traditions in favour of modern lifestyles, despite tourism. The challenge in Karamoja of “younger members moving away from practices” is reported (Monitor, 2023). Over tourism or inappropriate tourism: If visitor numbers or behaviour are not managed, heritage sites may be degraded or traditions disrupted; though in Karamoja, this risk is likely lower currently, given low volumes, but still relevant.
The Karamoja sub-region has distinctive features impacting how cultural tourism affects heritage preservation. Pastoralist, semi-nomadic traditions: The lifestyle of the Karamojong and associated architecture (manyattas), cattle herding, migration cycles, all differ from typical fixed heritage sites. Marginalisation and low infrastructure: Karamoja has among the lowest economic and service indices in Uganda, which means heritage preservation via tourism must contend with structural constraints (Ankole Times, 2024). Emerging tourism sector: Because tourism is still nascent, there is an opportunity to build sustainable models from the outset rather than retrofit problematic ones. Community sensitivity: Some traditions may be culturally sensitive, sacred, or under threat due to modernization, climate change, and youth migration; therefore, tourism must be designed carefully.
These factors make the “effect of cultural tourism on heritage preservation” in Karamoja particularly complex: While there is high potential for positive impact, there are also high risks if tourism development is not carefully managed.
In summary, Cultural tourism in Karamoja is gaining traction and has positive potential for heritage preservation, especially through community-based models, cultural festivals, and awareness campaigns. There is evidence of heritage being valued and utilized for tourism; e.g., rock painting sites, repatriation of artefacts, and community tourism hubs. However, risks and constraints are present: weak infrastructure, limited funding, potential for commodification, youth disengagement, and unequal benefit sharing. The literature specific to Karamoja remains limited in scholarly depth (compared to other areas), and thus, more empirical research is needed to evaluate the magnitude, community perceptions, and long-term heritage outcomes.
2.4.2 Environmental effects of cultural tourism
Cultural tourism in Karamoja, while offering socio-economic and heritage preservation benefits, can also have notable environmental effects. Increased visitation to cultural sites, natural landscapes, and wildlife areas—such as Kidepo Valley National Park and surrounding pastoral lands—may lead to soil erosion, vegetation damage, littering, and disturbance to local fauna if not properly managed (UNWTO, 2021). Additionally, the construction of tourism infrastructure, including lodges, roads, and cultural centers, can alter fragile ecosystems and water resources in semi-arid areas. However, when community-based tourism is carefully planned with sustainable practices, such as eco-friendly accommodations, regulated visitor numbers, and environmental education, these impacts can be mitigated while promoting both cultural and natural heritage conservation.
2.5 Theoretical Framework
This section situates the study in theory.
 Cultural Sustainability TheoryCultural sustainability theory emphasizes that for cultures to endure, they must adapt to change while retaining their core identity. As Harrison (2013) argues, heritage is not simply about the past but about how societies construct meaning in the present. Under this lens, cultural tourism can enable traditions to evolve in ways that make them relevant for communities today while maintaining continuity. Sustainable Livelihoods Framework. The sustainable livelihoods framework (SLF) considers how communities use assets (human, social, natural, financial, physical) to pursue sustainable living (DFID, 1999). In tourism studies, this framework has been applied to examine how community-based tourism can contribute to local livelihoods through tourism income, skill development, and empowerment (Ahebwa & van der Duim, 2013). This is relevant for Karamoja, where cultural tourism may enhance livelihood assets and thereby contribute to heritage preservation. Commodification & Authenticity Theory. From tourism and heritage scholarship, the commodification and authenticity debate warns that as cultures are brought into the tourism market, there is a risk of them being staged, altered, or simplified for tourist consumption. Dean MacCannell’s concept of the “staged authenticity” highlights how tourists’ expectations may drive communities to present a ‘tourist version’ of culture, which may undermine deeper meanings (MacCannell, 1973). Coupled with David McKercher & Hilary du Cros’s work on destructive commodification (McKercher & du Cros, 2020), these theories help frame the risks inherent in cultural tourism for heritage preservation. Conceptual Model. From the above theories, a conceptual model for the study can be summarised: Independent variable: Cultural tourism (community-based experiences, visitor engagement, tourism infrastructure). Mediating factors: Community participation, skills/training, benefit sharing, and authenticity management. Dependent variable: Preservation of local heritage and tradition (maintenance of practices, artifacts, community identity). Contextual / moderating factors: Regional context (Karamoja’s marginalisation, pastoralist culture), policy/legal frameworks, resources, infrastructure

2.6 Empirical Review
This section reviews empirical findings in the literature relevant to all three objectives. Empirical Evidence on Heritage Preservation. A number of studies have documented how heritage preservation works in diverse contexts. For example, Graham, Ashworth & Tunbridge (2016) argue that documentation, restoration, and community involvement are key to successful heritage conservation. Ndoro & Wijesuriya (2015) highlight in African contexts the importance of local custodianship and integration of intangible heritage. Challenges such as climate change, youth disengagement, and lack of funding are widely reported (UNESCO, 2022; British Council Cultural Protection Fund, 2023). Empirical Evidence on Cultural Tourism. In the tourism literature, cultural tourism is shown to generate economic, social, and cultural benefits when well managed. Richards (2020) documents that tourists increasingly seek immersive, meaningful cultural experiences. In Uganda, a national study on tourism destination competitiveness found that cultural heritage ranked highly among key factors affecting tourism performance, but infrastructure and funding were weak. Yet, empirical studies caution that benefits are unevenly distributed and authenticity may be compromised (Angella, 2024). Empirical Evidence on Cultural Tourism’s Effect on Heritage Preservation. Studies have begun to examine the direct links between cultural tourism and heritage preservation. A study in Uganda by Angella (2024) found that while cultural tourism raised awareness and helped generate income, some traditions were modified to suit tourist expectations. In the broader African context, Bello et al. (2020) found that community‐based cultural tourism initiatives supported both heritage transmission and local livelihoods—but success depended on local institutional capacity and empowerment.


2.7 Case Study: Community-based Cultural Tourism in Karamoja
Background
In the Africa Tourism and Environment Initiatives (ATEI) “Climate Innovation for Resilience in Karamoja (CLIRK)” project, in partnership with CARE International and funding from the Danish government via Danida, efforts were made in the Kapedo Sub-County, Karenga District, to build an agro tourism destination around the Kalimon Valley Dam area. The features of the case are that former cattle rustlers were retrained as tour guides, community scouts, and hospitality personnel, transforming erstwhile conflict actors into heritage and tourism custodians. The project integrates culture, nature conservation, climate resilience, and agro tourism. It involves local women, girls, and youth in planning and executing tourism activities. The initiative emphasizes community ownership, building capacity, and diversifying income from purely pastoral activities to tourism and Agro tourism. The impact on Heritage Preservation is that by creating new roles for local community members in tourism and heritage, the project helps maintain and transmit cultural practices (guiding, storytelling, cultural interpretation) that might otherwise fade. The cultural narratives and practices of the local communities are given value within the tourism experience, thus increasing local pride and motivation to preserve them. It fosters interaction between visitors and local culture, reinforcing the relevance of heritage rather than letting it remain stagnant or ignored. At the same time, the project addresses wider structural challenges (poverty, peace building, climate stress) – which indirectly strengthen conditions for heritage preservation by improving community resilience. The Uganda Tourism Board and its foreign partners oversee the Uganda Cultural Heritage Tourism Program, which aims to improve tourism while managing and conserving Uganda's cultural heritage sites. Karamoja cultural festivals are organized every year. The location has grown in popularity as a cultural tourism attraction thanks to community involvement, capacity building, and infrastructure upgrades. The initiative places a strong emphasis on the value of cooperation between national and local government agencies, as well as foreign organizations, to enhance cultural tourism and preserve historical sites (Ssebyala,2018). One study by the National Park Service (NPS) examines the relationship between cultural heritage and poverty eradication in Karamoja, arguing that cultural heritage can directly contribute to poverty reduction by generating income, creating employment, and improving living conditions. 
Challenges and Considerations. Though promising, such initiatives are still early-stage and need sustained investment, infrastructure, and market access. The broader region of Karamoja remains economically marginalised. Risk remains that tourism might shift cultural practices to serve visitors rather than the community’s own meanings. Authenticity and community benefit must be preserved. The documentation on long-term heritage outcomes is still limited; more empirical follow-up is required to ascertain the durability of preservation effects. Relevance to the Study. This case illustrates how cultural tourism can directly support heritage preservation in the Karamoja context by engaging local practices, building new capacities, and creating incentives for culture to be maintained. It thus provides empirical grounding for your third objective.

2.8 Future Directions and Recommendations: Moving forward, it is recommended that cultural tourism initiatives in Karamoja be strategically developed to ensure sustainable heritage preservation, with stronger community participation, capacity building, and equitable benefit-sharing. Future research should focus on longitudinal studies to assess the long-term impacts of tourism on both tangible and intangible cultural assets, and explore innovative methods to document and transmit indigenous knowledge to younger generations. Policymakers and stakeholders should prioritize infrastructure development, heritage protection laws, and educational programs that reinforce cultural identity while preventing the commodification of traditions. By integrating tourism, community empowerment, and heritage management, Karamoja can harness cultural tourism as a tool for economic growth, social cohesion, and the sustainable preservation of its unique cultural legacy.

2.10 Summary of Literature Gaps
While the literature reviewed above provides substantive insights, there are several gaps that my study can address: Context‐specific research for Karamoja: Much of the empirical work on cultural tourism and heritage preservation originates from global or other regional African contexts. In Karamoja, specifically, the literature remains thin. Longitudinal outcomes on heritage preservation: Few studies examine long‐term effects of tourism on heritage maintenance – whether traditions persist after initial interventions or cease once tourism interest declines. Community perceptions and internal value systems: There is limited qualitative research on how community members in Karamoja view cultural tourism and heritage preservation—how they define preservation, value participation, and see trade-offs. Authenticity and commodification in pastoralist heritage contexts: Much research deals with monuments, fixed heritage sites; less is done on migratory/pastoralist cultures (like the Karamojong) and how tourism affects intangible traditions. Benefit distribution and power dynamics: Research needs to probe how tourism income is shared, who controls resources, and how this impacts heritage custodianship. Infrastructure, policy and institutional frameworks: The role of regional infrastructure, policy support, funding flows, and organisational capacity in linking cultural tourism to heritage preservation in Karamoja is under explored.
